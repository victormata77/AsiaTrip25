Ancient temples, serene gardens, and timeless traditions

Welcome to Kyoto

Kyoto, Japan's cultural heart, offers a glimpse into the country's rich heritage with over 1,600 Buddhist temples, 400 Shinto shrines, and 17 UNESCO World Heritage sites. During our two-day stay, we'll explore vermilion torii gates, stroll through bamboo forests, witness the gleaming Golden Pavilion, and experience traditional culture through kimono wearing and geisha district walks. Kyoto embodies the essence of traditional Japan with its perfectly preserved temples, gardens, and time-honored customs.

Essential Information

Weather in August

  • • Average high: 33°C (91°F)
  • • Average low: 24°C (75°F)
  • • Humidity: Very high (75-85%)
  • • Rainfall: Frequent, summer is rainy season
  • • Daylight: Sunrise ~5:20 AM, Sunset ~6:50 PM

Kyoto's bowl-like geography can make it even hotter than Tokyo in summer. Carry water, wear light clothing, and plan indoor breaks during peak heat hours.

Getting Around

  • • One-Day Bus Pass: ¥700 (~€4.30) for unlimited city bus travel
  • • Subway: Two main lines (Karasuma and Tozai), convenient for certain sites
  • • Taxis: More prevalent than in Tokyo, reasonable for short trips
  • • Bicycle: Popular way to explore the relatively flat city center
  • • Walking: Many attractions in central Kyoto are walkable

Our hotel is located in the Shijo area, placing us centrally with great access to shopping, dining, and transit options. The subway station is a 3-minute walk away.

Must-Visit Sites

  • • Fushimi Inari Shrine: Thousands of vermilion torii gates
  • • Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion): Gold-leaf covered temple
  • • Arashiyama Bamboo Grove: Otherworldly bamboo forest
  • • Gion District: Traditional geisha district
  • • Nijo Castle: Former shogun residence with "nightingale floors"
  • • Kyoto Imperial Palace: Former residence of Japan's Imperial family

Our Itinerary

Our 2-day stay allows us to see the highlights, but Kyoto deserves more time. We've selected the most iconic sites that offer the quintessential Kyoto experience.

Kyoto Cuisine

  • • Kaiseki: Traditional multi-course haute cuisine
  • • Yudofu: Tofu hot pot (Kyoto specialty due to pure water)
  • • Yuba: Tofu skin, often served in soups or with soy sauce
  • • Tsukemono: Pickled vegetables with distinct Kyoto varieties
  • • Kyo-wagashi: Exquisite traditional sweets
  • • Matcha: High-grade green tea used in tea ceremonies

Where to Eat

Nishiki Market ("Kyoto's Kitchen") offers a wide variety of local specialties. Pontocho Alley features atmospheric restaurants along the Kamogawa River with outdoor dining in summer.

Cultural Experiences

  • • Kimono Rental: Experience Kyoto in traditional dress
  • • Maiko/Geisha Spotting: Early evening in Gion district
  • • Tea Ceremony: Participate in this refined ritual
  • • Traditional Crafts: Workshops for pottery, calligraphy
  • • Zen Gardens: Find serenity in meticulously designed spaces

Kimono Experience

We've scheduled a kimono/yukata rental experience on Day 2. This is a wonderful way to immerse in Japanese culture and get memorable photos in historic settings (¥6,000-10,000 per person).

Etiquette in Kyoto

  • • Temple Etiquette: Remove shoes where indicated, speak quietly
  • • Photography: Many temples prohibit photos of their interiors
  • • Geisha/Maiko: Do not chase or obstruct them; photos from a distance only
  • • Shrines: Wash hands at the purification fountain before entering
  • • Temples vs. Shrines: Temples are Buddhist, shrines are Shinto
  • • Dress Code: Modest attire for religious sites (shoulders/knees covered)

Important Note

Kyoto is more traditional than Tokyo, and locals particularly appreciate respectful behavior. Be especially mindful at religious sites and in the Gion district.

Culture & Fun Facts

🏯

Kyoto's Unique History

  • 🏛️ Imperial Capital: Kyoto served as Japan's capital and the emperor's residence from 794 to 1868 (over 1,000 years).
  • 🪖 WWII Preservation: Kyoto was deliberately spared from air raids during World War II due to its cultural significance, preserving its historic treasures.
  • 🏆 Cultural Treasure: The city is home to 17 UNESCO World Heritage Sites, more than any other city in Japan.
  • ⛩️ Religious Center: Kyoto has over 1,600 Buddhist temples and 400 Shinto shrines.
  • 🎨 Traditional Arts: Many traditional Japanese arts and crafts originated in Kyoto, including ikebana (flower arrangement), tea ceremony, and Kyo-yuzen dyeing.
  • 🏙️ Urban Planning: The city was designed using principles of Chinese feng shui, with a grid pattern modeled after the ancient Chinese capital of Chang'an (Xi'an).

⏳ Historical Timeline

  • • 794: Kyoto established as "Heian-kyo," Japan's new capital
  • • 794-1185: Heian Period (golden age of court aristocracy and culture)
  • • 1185-1333: Kamakura Period (rise of the samurai)
  • • 1333-1573: Muromachi Period (development of Zen culture)
  • • 1573-1603: Azuchi-Momoyama Period (unification of Japan)
  • • 1603-1868: Edo Period (Tokugawa shogunate)
  • • 1868: Capital moved to Tokyo during Meiji Restoration
👘

Geisha Culture in Kyoto

  • 💃 Geiko & Maiko: In Kyoto, fully-trained geisha are called "geiko" (meaning "arts child"), while apprentices are "maiko" (dancing child).
  • 📚 Rigorous Training: Maiko typically begin training at 15-16 and spend about 5 years as apprentices before becoming full geiko.
  • 🎭 Traditional Arts: Geiko study traditional Japanese arts including dance, music (especially shamisen), conversation, games, and tea ceremony.
  • 🏮 Hanamachi: Kyoto has five geisha districts called "hanamachi" (flower towns), with Gion Kobu being the most famous.
  • 💄 Appearance: Maiko wear more elaborate kimono, hairstyles, and makeup than geiko. The iconic white makeup has its origins in stage lighting before electricity.
  • 📊 Modern Numbers: While there were once thousands of geisha in Kyoto, today there are only about 100 geiko and 100 maiko actively working.

👀 Spotting Authentic Geiko & Maiko

The best time to see geiko and maiko is early evening (5:30-6:00 PM) as they travel to their appointments in Gion, particularly on Hanami-koji Street. Be respectful—observe from a distance and do not chase or obstruct them. Remember that many women in kimono are tourists in costume, not actual geiko or maiko.

🎭

Kyoto's Major Festivals

  • 🏮 Gion Matsuri: One of Japan's most famous festivals, held throughout July with the highlight being the grand procession of floats on July 17th.
  • 🌿 Aoi Matsuri: Held on May 15th, featuring a procession of people in Heian Period costumes from the Imperial Palace to Shimogamo and Kamigamo Shrines.
  • 👑 Jidai Matsuri: The "Festival of Ages" on October 22nd showcases Kyoto's history with a costume procession representing different historical periods.
  • 🔥 Gozan no Okuribi: Also known as "Daimonji," this August 16th event features five giant bonfires lit on mountains surrounding Kyoto to guide ancestral spirits home after Obon.
  • 👹 Setsubun: February 3rd ceremonies at Yoshida Shrine include the throwing of beans to drive away evil spirits.

📅 August Events

During our visit in August, we might witness preparations for Gozan no Okuribi (August 16th) and potentially smaller local festivals. August in Kyoto also features evening illuminations at certain temples and special night openings to combat the summer heat.

💡

Did You Know?

  • ✨ The Golden Pavilion (Kinkaku-ji) is actually covered in gold leaf, with the top two floors completely gilded with an estimated 20 pounds of gold.
  • 🔤 The name "Kyoto" is actually an anagram of "Tokyo"—when the capital moved to Tokyo (meaning "Eastern Capital"), Kyoto (meaning "Capital City") maintained its cultural importance.
  • 🎮 Nintendo was founded in Kyoto in 1889 as a playing card company and still has its headquarters in the city.
  • 🌡️ Kyoto is surrounded by mountains on three sides, which creates a basin effect that makes it one of the hottest cities in Japan during summer.
  • 🏯 Unlike Tokyo, which was heavily bombed during World War II, Kyoto was deliberately removed from the target list due to its cultural significance—the U.S. Secretary of War Henry Stimson had honeymooned in Kyoto and appreciated its historical importance.
  • ⛩️ The torii gates at Fushimi Inari Shrine number over 10,000 and would stretch over 4 kilometers if placed end to end.
  • 💧 Kyoto is known for its superior water quality from natural springs, which contributed to the development of its famous tea ceremony culture, sake brewing, and tofu production.
  • 🔊 Nijo Castle's "nightingale floors" were specifically designed to squeak when walked upon as a security measure against intruders.
🎨

Traditional Kyoto Arts & Crafts

  • 🧵 Kyo-yuzen: A dyeing technique for creating elaborate designs on kimono fabric, developed in the 17th century.
  • 🏺 Kiyomizu-yaki: Pottery made in the Kiyomizu area, known for its bright colors and intricate designs.
  • 👘 Nishijin-ori: Exquisite silk weaving tradition dating back over 1,200 years, used for high-quality kimono and obi (sashes).
  • 🍽️ Kyo-shikki: Kyoto-style lacquerware, often featuring gold or silver inlay (maki-e) decoration.
  • 👧 Kyoto Dolls: Traditional handcrafted dolls that represent the refined aesthetic of the imperial court.
  • 🌸 Kyo-sensu: Decorative folding fans with designs reflecting the seasons and Kyoto landscapes.

🛍️ Souvenir Shopping

The best places to purchase authentic Kyoto crafts include Shinmonzen and Furumonzen Streets in Gion (known as "museum streets"), traditional specialty shops around Nishiki Market, and the Kyoto Handicraft Center. Look for the official "Kyo" seal certifying authentic Kyoto-made products.

🪴

Kyoto's Famous Gardens

  • 🧘 Zen Gardens: Kyoto is famous for its karesansui (dry landscape) gardens featuring carefully arranged rocks, pruned trees, and raked gravel or sand.
  • ⛰️ Ryoanji Temple: Home to Japan's most famous rock garden, consisting of 15 rocks arranged on white gravel—a masterpiece of Zen minimalism.
  • 🥈 Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion): Features the unique "Sea of Silver Sand," a meticulously raked sand garden with a sculpted sand cone called "Moon Viewing Platform."
  • 🌳 Tenryu-ji: A UNESCO World Heritage site with a stunning landscape garden designed by Zen master Muso Soseki that harmonizes with the Arashiyama mountains.
  • 🌿 Saiho-ji: Known as the "Moss Temple," featuring over 120 species of moss creating a lush green carpet. Requires advance reservation.
  • 🍁 Shisen-do: A secluded temple garden with a beautiful combination of Japanese and Chinese influences, particularly famous for its autumn colors.

Japanese gardens are designed as microcosms of nature, arranged to reveal new perspectives from different viewing points. Each element has symbolic meaning, and gardens change dramatically with the seasons.
